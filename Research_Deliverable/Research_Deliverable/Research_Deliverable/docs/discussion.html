Disability in the Digital World: How Representation Varies Across Cultures and Languages

Section 4: Discussion of Results

Discussion

    In summary, Russia and Mexico, the more collectivist cultures in this research, displayed both binding and individualizing moral foundations in news headlines, thereby showing some similarity with the United States. The Mexico corpus demonstrated that the Care-Harm (an individualizing value), and Purity-Sanctity foundation (a binding value), were the most common when a disability term was present. The Russian corpus showed that the fairness-injustice foundation (individualizing), and authority-subversion foundation (binding) were invoked the most when a disability term was present. Lastly, when a disability term was present, the moral values of care-harm and fairness-injustive appeared the most. Our results suggest that the topic of disability is indeed moralized and that this is true across cultures: at least one of the Moral Foundations values appeared when a disability term was present in a news headline in each of the US, Russia, and Mexico. Therefore, all three countries refer to disability with a sense of “right” and “wrong” and moral responsibility in the media.

    For Russia, the two foundations that showed up the most were a combination of an individualizing and binding value, most likely because of its technological advancements as a “western” power. The first foundation that appeared the most was the “Fairness and Injustice” value, which probably referred to the disadvantages that people with disabilities face in Russia. The second was “Authority and Subversion”, which may mean that there is a value in either the higher authority, such as the heads of state or the country, in professionals in general, or even “higher” members of a family like adults or members who are “more able”. In this case, the latter is the most probable considering that Russia is a collectivist society, which still emphasizes a family’s or a community’s needs over the individual.

    For Mexico, the two foundations most apparent were also a combination of individualizing and binding values, possibly for the same reasons as Russia. The two foundations were the “Care and Harm” and “Purity and Sanctity” values. The “Care and Harm” value for Mexico may mean that there is an expectation to aid the lesser-abled members of a family or a community, thus emphasizing care in news headlines. The “Purity and Sanctity” value is due to the homogeneity that a collectivist society expects of its individuals. This may mean that there is a certain expectation for a tight-knit group of individuals who are similar to each other, therefore may be referring to people with disabilities in a negative way. However, this value can also have a more positive outlook on these individuals by emphasizing their need because they don’t abide by the social norms, therefore calling its members to action.

    Lastly, the United States, the most individualist country in this research, demonstrated strictly individualizing values: Care/Harm and Fairness/Injustice. This was expected due to its emphasis on breaking the status quo and praising the success of the individual.

    These differences in representation, especially in the news which is a very powerful source of cultural representations, may have downstream consequences for differences in policy, law, and even opportunities for people who are differently-abled. For instance, the United States has had a much more open discussion of disability than most other countries, and there are many schools and civil society actors that aim to support the differently-abled. However, other countries such as Russia and Mexico who are more collectivist, may exhibit less opportunities for support that go beyond the immediate family or even schools for children.

Limitations

    Some of the corpora of the countries in this research had a limited number of media outlets in comparison to the United States, which had the most one by far. Therefore, this research did not encompass all news headlines in Russia and Mexico which may have limited our results. Additionally, the countries selected did not maximize the difference across the individualist-collectivist spectrum. The United States is one of the most individualist countries, but Russia and Mexico are more in the middle of the spectrum (as seen in the results of this research). Other countries that may have been included in this research are South or East Asian countries such as India or China. (Triandis, 2015) Because our test is more conservative, it’s very surprising that there is a cross-cultural difference despite not maximizing the spectrum.

Future Research

    Future research should investigate the direct effects on the members of the community who have disabilities. This research mainly focused on the representation of this group of individuals through a journalist’s lens, who may or may not have a disability themselves. Therefore, focusing more on personal experiences may lead to a more comprehensive comparison of sentiments and representations of people with disabilities. Additionally, another way to investigate further this cross-cultural research is to collect qualitative perspectives of community members not just within the United States, Mexico, and Russia, but more broadly across continents and countries.
