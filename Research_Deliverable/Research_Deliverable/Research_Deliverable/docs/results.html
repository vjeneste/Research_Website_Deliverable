Disability in the Digital World: How Representation Varies Across Cultures and Languages

Section 3: Results

United States of America

    An important feature to note about the United States of America is that it is an individualist culture (Shkodriani & Gibbons, 1995), which is defined as believing in individual freedom and emphasizing individual successes and self-reliance (Awad et al., 2018). Additionally, individualist cultures focus more on separation of self and less on allegiance to society or family and competition. Typically, individualism and capitalism coincide, often focusing on competition. (Awad et al., 2018; Realo & Allik, 1999)

Corpus and Analytic Procedure

    The data for the U.S. were drawn from the News on the Web (NOW) Corpus, a large-scale archive containing approximately 23 billion words from online magazines and newspapers published between 2010 and the present across twenty English-speaking countries. All analyses were conducted in R (version 4.3) using text-processing and data-analysis packages such as tidyverse and quanteda. Each article was treated as a single text document. Texts were tokenized, converted to lowercase, and stripped of punctuation and stop words before dictionary lookup.

    Two dictionaries were used: one related to disability and one related to moral foundations. Both dictionaries were expanded and refined using large-language-model (LLM) assistance (Ai2’s OLMo-2 32B) to ensure comprehensive lexical coverage, including synonyms, plural forms, and regional variants. Examples of disability-related terms include amputee, cane, invalid (now considered derogatory), and visually impaired. Moral-foundation terms included words such as compassion, kindness, sympathy, victim, and unharmed.

    For each article, we computed binary indicators for whether any disability term and/or any moral-foundation term appeared. We then estimated the mean probability of moral-foundation word occurrence conditional on the presence of a disability term. Results indicated that the probability of a moral-foundation term was higher in texts that mentioned disability than in those that did not. When broken down by moral-foundation category (Harm/Care, Fairness/Cheating, Authority/Subversion, Loyalty/Betrayal, Purity/Degradation), the largest differences occurred for the Harm and Fairness foundations. The Authority foundation showed a slight increase when a disability term was present, but its 95% confidence interval overlapped zero, indicating a nonsignificant difference (p > .05).

    In summary, disability discourse in U.S. news sources most strongly co-occurred with moral language linked to individualist concerns—harm and fairness—consistent with the broader individualist orientation of American culture.

Mexico

    Collectivism, rather than individualism, is an important feature of Mexican culture (Shkodriani & Gibbons, 1995). Collectivism emphasizes group harmony and obligations to family, community, and tradition rather than personal autonomy (Awad et al., 2018; Gregory & Munch, 1997). Moral foundations commonly associated with collectivism include authority/respect, loyalty/betrayal, and purity/sanctity.

    The same analytic framework used for the U.S. data was applied to Mexican news texts. Two parallel Spanish-language dictionaries were developed—one for disability and one for moral foundations—by translating the English terms, expanding them using LLM-assisted generation, and validating them against native-speaker judgments. Although the NOW Corpus contains Mexican sources, we supplemented it with a Mexico-specific news corpus to increase coverage and used the Spanish originals rather than machine-translated texts.

    As in the U.S. analysis, the probability of moral-foundation language was compared across texts with and without disability terms using mean differences and bootstrap-estimated confidence intervals. On average, the proportion of moral-foundation terms was significantly higher when a disability term appeared (error = 0.0266). The largest increases occurred for the Harm and Purity foundations, whereas Fairness, Ingroup, and Authority showed negligible or nonsignificant differences.

    These findings suggest that in Mexican media, discussions of disability often invoke moral language centered on care and purity—consistent with collectivist themes of interdependence, protection, and moral cleanliness.

Russia

    Russian culture is also broadly collectivist but differs from Mexican collectivism in its stronger emphasis on authority and respect for the state rather than familial duty (Martz et al., 2009; Realo & Allik, 1999). This orientation reflects the legacy of the Soviet period, when loyalty to social order and obedience to authority were strongly enforced.

    As no pre-existing Russian-language dictionaries for disability or moral foundations were available, we constructed them by translating the English source dictionaries using OLMo-2 (32B) and then verifying each translation with a native Russian speaker. These dictionaries were applied to the Lenta Corpus, a large-scale collection of Russian news texts. The same tokenization and lookup procedures were used as in prior analyses.

    Results indicated that the “Fairness/Injustice” (individualizing) and “Authority/Subversion” (binding) foundations appeared most frequently in texts mentioning disability, whereas “Care/Harm,” “Purity/Sanctity,” and “Ingroup” terms appeared less often.

    This pattern aligns with Russia’s socio-cultural emphasis on respect for authority and social conformity, suggesting that disability discourse there is framed in terms of fairness and social order rather than compassion or purity.

Cross-Cultural Comparison

    Across all three countries, results reflect their distinct cultural orientations. The U.S., a highly individualist society, showed the strongest associations between disability and care or fairness language. Mexico, a familial and collectivist society, emphasized care and purity foundations. Russia, shaped by state-oriented collectivism, linked disability most strongly to fairness and authority. Together, these findings demonstrate that the moral framing of disability varies systematically with cultural values, highlighting how collective versus individualist moral concerns shape public discourse.
